# How Vancouver & Victoria parents raise kids who get into UBC, UofT, and Waterloo

Profiles of recent (2023–2025) high-school graduates from Vancouver, BC and Victoria, BC who received offers from the University of British Columbia, the University of Toronto, and/or the University of Waterloo, with what they did in high school. Compiled from public news features (school district press releases, university announcements, scholarship organization profiles, and local news).

A note on what's available publicly: GPAs are rarely published — Canadian admission write-ups tend to publicize extracurriculars and awards rather than top-six averages. Where the source did not state a GPA, the field below says "not stated." All profiled students won at least one major scholarship (Schulich Leader, Loran, UBC entrance awards), which is how they ended up in news coverage in the first place — they sit at the top end of the admit pool, not the median. The "Description" field summarizes what the source reports about how the student got the offer.

The Vancouver/Victoria signal here is heavier than UofT signal — most BC-side news coverage celebrates UBC and Waterloo offers because those are where local high-achievers most often land. UofT-bound BC students are featured less often.

## What patterns repeat across these families

Reading these profiles together, several patterns emerge that are consistent across BC parents whose kids landed top offers.

**The Schulich Leader pipeline is the single most reliable BC route into competitive UBC programs.** Every BC high school can submit exactly one Schulich nominee per year. Parents and students who understand this start early — in Grade 9 or 10 — building the science-fair / national-competition / nonprofit-founder portfolio that beats out the rest of the school. By Grade 11, the strongest candidates are already known to school counsellors. The award itself is portable across 20 partner universities (including UBC, Waterloo, and UofT), so a Schulich win functionally chooses the student's university for them.

**Multi-year science-fair commitment, not just one-time entry.** Patrick Huynh, Maya Achuthan, and several others did regional/national science fair across multiple years, with progressively more sophisticated projects. Maya literally said she'd "known about the Schulich for the last four years" because she was already doing the Canada-Wide Science Fair in Grade 9. Parents in the data tend to start kids on regional science fair (Greater Vancouver Regional Science Fair) or VanCity Heritage Fair early in middle school, then escalate to provincial/national.

**Founder/scale stories beat join/attend stories.** Jeanette Guo grew her school's engineering club into a district-wide hub (SHEESH). David Yang founded a coding nonprofit that reached 800+ kids. Adam Chen founded a mountain-biking nonprofit. Alexander Zhong built a tutoring website. The Schulich and Loran selectors all reward the student who started the thing, not the student who joined it.

**Public schools dominate this list, not private ones.** SMUS (private, Victoria) appears once for Maya Achuthan — and notably it was their first-ever Schulich winner in the school's century-plus history. The other 12 profiles are public-school students from David Thompson, Sir Winston Churchill, Britannia, Windermere, Hugh McRoberts, Richmond Secondary, Esquimalt, Rockridge, Delta Secondary, and Seaquam. The Vancouver public-school catchment that probably matters most is whatever school is willing to nominate a strong Grade 12 — which makes school-counsellor relationships the actual lever.

**Two clear paths exist for the very-early-acceleration approach.** The Transition Program for Gifted Students (TPGS, public, housed at UBC) and the University Transition Program (UTP, public, also at UBC) both compress high school into 2–3 years and let identified gifted students enter UBC at 15. David Yang and Leo Xu both came through these programs and won Schulich scholarships. For parents seriously considering the radical-acceleration path, the entry decision happens around Grade 6–7 with gifted assessment, not later.

**Civic/policy work can substitute for contest math (but only if it's loud).** Chloe Nguyen got into Waterloo Systems Design Engineering by co-founding Climate Education Reform BC — campaigning to change the BC curriculum — rather than via the typical CCC/Euclid contest path. The Loran specifically values character and service, but Waterloo's AIF can be carried by a strong, specific civic-policy story too.

**Britannia Secondary keeps producing Loran scholars from East Van.** Britannia has been the catchment for two consecutive Loran Scholar wins (2024 Chloe Huang, 2025 Magfira Ali) plus a 2026 finalist (Anisa Abduhamedova). For a school in a lower-income catchment that's not on the typical "best Vancouver high school" lists, that's an outlier and suggests strong staff-side mentorship around the Loran nomination process specifically. Parents in that catchment shouldn't assume their kid needs to transfer out.

**Parents are explicitly named in several of these stories.** Maya Achuthan credits her tech-entrepreneur parents (Ajay and Priya). Valerie Nguyen explicitly cites her parents' valuing of education for security. Several Delta students profiled in the same source thank their parents for driving them to practices and games and "believing in me even during challenging times." The thread is not pressure — it's logistics-plus-belief: parents who drove, paid for things, believed it was possible, and let the kid pick the specific direction.

**What's notably absent from these stories.** None of the BC profiles mention private SAT prep, Kumon-style early acceleration, or paid admission consultants. None mention competitive math contests as the primary route — Sophie Jiang and Adam Chen had contest results, but they're the exception. None mention private schools other than SMUS. The dominant route, repeatedly, is: pick a domain you actually care about, do it for years, scale it past your school, and get nominated for the Schulich or the Loran.

## How to use this document

If you're a parent in Vancouver or Victoria thinking about university outcomes, the actionable signal in these profiles is the timeline:

By end of elementary school, decide whether you're pursuing the gifted-acceleration track (TPGS / UTP — apply Grade 6–7) or the regular track. By Grade 9, the regular-track student should be doing science fair or its equivalent and have at least one extracurricular they can credibly call their own. By Grade 10, that extracurricular should have grown beyond its starting size. By Grade 11, the school counsellor should know your child by name as a serious Schulich/Loran candidate. By Grade 12 fall, the AIF / Personal Profile / Schulich application is being written, and the strongest candidates have a multi-year, founder-shaped story to tell.

The students profiled here didn't all do the same thing. They all did *one* thing, for a long time, that grew bigger than them.

---

## Caveats

These profiles are biased toward winners of major scholarships. The median UBC, Waterloo, or UofT admit from Vancouver/Victoria does not look like this — they typically get in on a strong top-6 average (low-to-mid 90s for competitive programs) plus a decent Personal Profile / AIF, without ever winning a Schulich. The profiles above describe the *top of the distribution*, because that's the part of the distribution that gets news coverage. If your goal is "any UBC admit" rather than "Schulich Leader at UBC," you need much less than this — but the underlying parental moves (start early, support a real interest, scale it past the school, find the school counsellor) still apply.
